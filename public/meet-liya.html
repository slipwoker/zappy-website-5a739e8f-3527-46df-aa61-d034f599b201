Industrial Designer. Origami Maker. Jewelry Artisan.

With over two decades of industrial design experience, Liya brings a rare precision to handmade origami jewelry. Each piece is an iteration—sketched, folded, refined—until form and wearability meet. What began as a study in paper geometry has evolved into a collection of wearable sculptures that honor both discipline and craft.

Every earring, pendant, and bracelet carries the fingerprint of a designer who understands structure, balance, and the quiet beauty of restraint. These aren't just accessories; they're small monuments to the deliberate act of making.

A Practice Built on Iteration

Twenty years of industrial design taught me to think in constraints—material limits, ergonomic thresholds, the physics of how things hold together under stress. When I began folding paper five years ago, I brought that same mindset to the table: What can a single sheet bear? How does geometry translate into wearable form? Every piece starts as a prototype, tested for balance, durability, and the way it moves with the body.

Origami became my geometry language—a way to express structure without welding or casting. Each fold is a decision about tension and release, about where light catches an edge. I learned quickly that paper logic doesn't always survive the leap to metal or polymer; some folds collapse, others spring open. The handmade jewelry I create now is the result of hundreds of failed experiments, each one teaching me which angles hold, which seams need reinforcement, which forms feel right against skin.

Hand-finishing matters because machines can't read a fold's intention. I sand each edge by feel, checking how it sits against a collarbone or wrist. I test clasps under real-world conditions—opening a door, typing, reaching overhead. Industrial design taught me that ergonomics aren't optional; they're the difference between jewelry that lives in a drawer and jewelry that becomes part of someone's daily ritual. Every piece I sell at Nachlat Binyamin has passed that test.

Small-batch integrity means I can trace every decision back to my hands. I know which supplier milled the brass, which finish resists tarnish best in Tel Aviv's humidity, why one fold pattern works for earrings but fails as a pendant. This isn't romantic craft nostalgia—it's quality control at the most granular level. When you buy a piece, you're buying two decades of industrial problem-solving compressed into a single, wearable geometry.

Influences:I draw from brutalist architecture (raw material honesty), paper engineering (structural elegance), and the Japanese concept ofma—the space between folds that gives a form its breathing room.

What makes a fold "work":It has to hold its shape under gravity, flex without cracking, and create intentional shadow lines. A successful fold feels inevitable, like it couldn't have been any other way.

Commitment to small batches:I produce 8–12 pieces per design, then retire the pattern. This keeps my practice exploratory and ensures each buyer owns something genuinely limited.

Inside the Studio

Every piece begins with careful attention to detail—hand tools, paper studies, prototype boards, and meticulous finishing. Each fold is tested, each texture refined, until the design feels just right. The studio is a space of quiet focus, where traditional origami craft meets industrial design precision.

Design Principles

Every piece I create reflects decades of industrial design training merged with the meditative practice of origami. These principles guide how I approach each fold, each connection, each finished piece.

Lightweight geometry

Origami teaches economy of form—every fold serves a purpose. I translate this into jewelry that feels substantial yet weightless, where geometric precision creates visual interest without burden. The paper's memory becomes the structure.

Wearability first

Twenty years of industrial design taught me that beauty must serve function. Each piece is tested for comfort, balance, and movement. The clasp must close easily. The weight must distribute evenly. Form follows how you'll actually wear it.

Craft as precision

Hand-crafted doesn't mean imprecise. Every crease is measured, every angle calculated. The craft lies in translating mathematical accuracy into organic beauty—where the hand's warmth meets the mind's rigor, creating pieces that are both intentional and alive.
